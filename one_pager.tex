Twenty-two players are tracked at 10 frames a second. Given everything up to the throw, the task is to
predict where the targeted players will be for each frame until the ball lands (sometimes forty-plus
frames ahead), scored by RMSE on the $(x,y)$ coordinates over roughly 14{,}000 plays. The catch that
shapes the whole project: training is the 2023 season while the hidden test is a \emph{later} one, so a
model that memorizes 2023 does not travel. A constant-velocity baseline scores 1.645 and sets the
floor.

\section*{The model}
A compact CNN$+$Transformer, roughly 1.4M parameters early on and 4.9M at full size (\gh{train.py}).
Each of the 22 players becomes one token: a \textbf{depthwise 1-D CNN} reads that player's last 20
frames on its own (position, speed, acceleration, the sine and cosine of heading and orientation, and
vectors to the ball's landing spot and the intended receiver). A \textbf{pre-norm SwiGLU Transformer} then lets the tokens attend across each
other, since a defender's path is largely a response to the receiver's, and a convolutional head
decodes each into a per-frame \emph{displacement} plus its own variance out to 48 frames. Training uses
a Gaussian negative-log-likelihood (which penalizes being both wrong and overconfident) with velocity
and acceleration auxiliary heads, heavy geometric augmentation (random rotation, mirroring, early
starts), EMA weight averaging, and a horizontal-flip test-time average.

\section*{Autoresearch: turning tuning into an experiment loop}
Rather than hand-tune, I ran the project as a Karpathy-style autoresearch loop, a discipline about not
fooling yourself. \gh{prepare.py} is \emph{frozen}: it builds the data and defines the RMSE metric and
the validation split, so nothing I optimize can quietly change how it is judged. \gh{train.py} is the
only file I edit, each run gets the same fixed wall-clock budget so two experiments are comparable, and
every result is re-scored from saved predictions and logged with its git commit to \gh{results.tsv}.
Twelve overnight hours produced \textbf{33 tracked experiments}, and the biggest lever it found was
that the published recipe over-weights the velocity/acceleration loss for my training budget: cutting
that weight while raising the learning rate moved the model from 0.94 to 0.63.

\section*{From holdout to leaderboard}
My champion scored 0.478 on held-out weeks but 0.579 on the real test, because the holdout was later
weeks of the \emph{same} 2023 season while the test is a new one. So I pointed the loop at the
leaderboard itself and swept learning rate, model width and depth, the auxiliary-loss weight,
augmentation, the decoder, and the ensemble makeup. Scaling to full size and 37k steps took a single
model to 0.536, and fold ensembling to 0.534. The largest jump was not a hyperparameter: a parity test
replayed raw plays through the submission path and the local scorer, they disagreed by 0.05, and the
diff showed one feature (frames-to-predict) was zeroed for every unscored player at inference. Fixing
that one line dropped the identical ensemble to 0.475.

\section*{The final model}
The submission is a four-way ensemble of the full network, each member 4.9M parameters (64 channels per
feature, a 256-wide 3-layer Transformer) trained on a different 2023 fold. Three members integrate
per-frame displacements and one uses a direct head, which decorrelates their errors. The private RMSE
is \textbf{0.47458}, which lands \textbf{4th of roughly 1{,}900 teams} (behind only 0.4634, 0.4648, and
0.4658) and well inside the top-20 cutoff of 0.5046. The gap left to first is single-model quality, not
ensembling.
